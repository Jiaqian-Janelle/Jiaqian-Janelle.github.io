## Why I'm watching

The canonical intro to startups, taught by founders who've actually built billion-dollar
companies. A great foundation before going deeper.

## Lecture 1 — Ideas, Products, Team & Execution (Sam Altman)

### Deciding to start a startup
1. Startups aren't clearly the best way to maximize financial reward or impact. You might do
   better by joining an established company.
2. Passion is really important, since we just talked about how hard it is to start a company.
   The idea itself is really valuable, and it also implies that you're the best person to bring
   that idea into the world. If you're not the best person, then that best person's out there,
   and they're probably going to out-compete you and create something even more valuable.

### Ideas
4. The most successful startups are not derivatives. They are not a copy of something else that
   was working pretty well. Most people try to start a copy of whatever worked last year — I
   don't know how many people started a Facebook clone in the year after Facebook.
5. Start noticing problems in your own life. The great advantage you have as students is that
   young people tend to be on the forefront of technology — you can predict the great wave
   before it happens. This idea of the great wave is the most important concept to find good
   ideas out there.
6. Starting a company is always hard, and it's about equally hard no matter what you do. But if
   you start a hard company — if you inspire passionate people, if you're building general AI or
   supersonic airplanes or nuclear power — you'll have a lot more people excited about that than
   another derivative idea.

### Co-founders
3. Work with someone you've known a long time and like. Discuss as many of the details as
   possible to make sure you're on the same page, because those conversations get a lot harder
   later.
7. If you have a shared history and a bond with someone, you keep going anyway because you don't
   want to let your friend down. Select for determination — it's the most important value in a
   co-founder I've ever been able to identify, and it's not the thing people look for the most.
   Startups are really hard; determined people are the ones that make it work. Startups are about
   not giving up. Our best founders say things like, "I always figure it out, I never give up."
8. Think values first, aptitude second, and specific skills third. Most people go in the
   opposite order — "I need a co-founder who knows JavaScript and X and Y." Really you want
   someone that matches your values, especially determination; someone with a lot of potential
   and aptitude; and finally specific skills. And you want someone humble and not entitled.

### Product
9. It is more important to have a small number of users that love you than a lot of users that
   like you. Almost all startups get this wrong. You have two choices: go deep and narrow — a
   small number of users that really love you, then find more of them and broaden the appeal — or
   have a lot of people that use it once or twice, kind of like it, and try to get them more
   engaged over time.
10. A good indicator is retention and frequency of use. In the early days you actually shouldn't
    track absolute growth in number of users — track how often they're using it. Get good at
    analyzing your metrics: is this a user I'm retaining and that's using it frequently? How do I
    compare to other products in my space? That's a good early indicator of users that love you.
11. Nothing but a great product will save you. We'll talk about a lot of other things in this
    course and they all matter, but if you don't get this one right — if you don't make a great
    product — the thing is still not going to work. Better still is users spontaneously telling
    their friends to buy your product.
12. Talk to your users. You can't build a great product in a vacuum — you need a small number of
    users to talk to and iterate with. People are going to be too nice to you, so find out
    exactly what they like, watch them use it, figure out where they're doing weird things. Ask:
    "Have you recommended this to anyone else? If not, why not? Have you paid me yet? If not, why
    not? What would that take?" Dig into specific features, what they used before, and times they
    stopped using your product for another.

### Getting your first users
- Everyone thinks they'll put up a website, tell one person, and it'll take off like wildfire.
  That's not what usually happens.
- Email people you already know and ask them to be your customers. Call in all the favors with
  anyone you can think of. If it's a paid product, actually charge — people are inclined to do
  you favors and be too nice, so charging keeps them honest.
- Research people you think might use your product and email them to try it. Conversion rates are
  low — maybe two to three percent — so you'll have to do this to more. Most people want to be
  helpful and probably will.
13. You can do social media, Hacker News, forums, press, whatever. If this is your growth
    strategy, find a way for it to be ongoing — not one big pop and then go away. Most people
    find it works once, then ask the journalist to write about them again with nothing changed,
    and the journalist says no.
14. The laziest and least impressive thing you can do is buy ads on Facebook or Google and point
    people to your website. This is not what I recommend — I don't know of any startup that's
    gotten big starting this way. I include it because it's the idea most people try.
- Getting to know your users really well is important. The best founders do customer support
  themselves. They go visit their users, sit in their office, and in the case of Airbnb, go live
  with them.

### The iteration cycle
15. Talk to customers, understand the pain point, build a product to address it, get it in front
    of a user, ask them, see what they do, and repeat. The law of compound growth being what it
    is: if you can get two percent better every iteration cycle, and your cycle is every four
    hours or every four weeks, compounded over a few years, you get to very different places.

### Team & hiring
16. Stay lean until everything is working really well. This is somewhat controversial. In the
    early days you're experimenting and zigzagging — you want to be a fast little speedboat that
    can turn the whole company on a dime, and you can't do that if you're big. Cash burn aside,
    the flexibility of a company decreases with the square of the number of employees. Stay
    really small until you're sure things are working; once they are, you can get really big, and
    you'll need to.
17. You'll know once users are begging you for your product, and you're convinced you have great
    product-market fit — that's when you can really start to scale up the company.
18. Resist the urge to hire mediocre people. As Nokosta (speaking later) says: "The team you
    build is the company you build." If you build a team of great people and have a product
    people love, you have a ninety-something percent chance of success. Both are really hard to
    do and they're independent variables — don't ignore the team point.
19. The best CEOs I know spend huge amounts of their time recruiting and retaining their talent.

### Execution
20. Relentless execution. You have to keep going and keep going, do things perfectly, get all the
    details right, and care too much about every experience a customer has with your company.
21. Startups are about not giving up to a degree most people don't have a good intuitive
    understanding for. One of the very best companies in the last YC batch applied seven times
    before getting in. Most people, you reject them six times and they won't apply a seventh.
    It's that last time you get pushed down and don't think you have enough energy to get back up
    — that's when it finally works.
22. Remember, if this is a ten-year marathon, you have a fiduciary duty to your shareholders to
    take care of yourself. Some people treat a startup as an all-nighter and don't do a good job
    taking care of their health.

### Mission & communication
23. A clear mission. You don't have to figure this out on day one, but all the most successful
    startups figure out a really important mission pretty quickly — within the first year or two
    at the outside. It is this mission that gets people to join them, that drives them and the
    founders, and that gets the media to write about them.
24. Be a great communicator. You need to clearly think and clearly communicate this clear
    mission. That's what convinces people to come help you, and how you build this idea into a
    huge company with all these people who really love your product.
