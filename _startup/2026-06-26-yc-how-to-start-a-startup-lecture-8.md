## Why I'm watching

The whole lecture circles one idea: in the early days you win by doing manual,
unsustainable, founder-intensive things that bigger competitors can't or won't do. Three
founders show what that looks like in practice — from being your own delivery driver to
manually pitching reporters one by one.

## Part 1 — How DoorDash Got Started (Stanley Tang)

### Find the need
1. Start from a problem you keep hearing about. The delivery idea came from interviewing a
   Palo Alto shop owner who was personally driving orders she couldn't fulfill — then
   validating it across ~150–200 more small business owners who all said the same thing.
2. If something obvious hasn't been solved, ask why. Their answer in hindsight: **mobile**.
   Everyone now carries a phone, so you can run delivery off an on-demand pool of independent
   contractors instead of owning trucks and hiring full-time drivers.

### Test the hypothesis cheaply
1. Treat your startup idea like an experiment. They had no trucks, no infrastructure, no way
   to "spin up a delivery company overnight" — so they designed the smallest possible test.
2. Launch fast. In about an hour they built PaloAltoDelivery.com: PDF menus pulled off the
   web, their personal cell number at the bottom, and nothing else. Ugly, simple, live.
3. The signal that you've found a real need is that people put up with a terrible experience
   to use it. Orders went 1 → 2 → 5 → 7 → 10, almost entirely by word of mouth (he sent one
   email to his dorm; that was the marketing).

### Do things that don't scale
1. At the start, none of the "real" infrastructure matters. They were the drivers (delivering
   between classes), the customer support (taking calls during lecture), and the marketers
   (handing out flyers on University Avenue).
2. Hack the back end together. Square to charge customers, Google Docs to track orders, Find
   My Friends to locate drivers. (Square once froze their account on suspicion of money
   laundering because of the rapid stream of small $15–20 charges.)
3. Unscalable work makes you an expert in your own business. Driving taught them how delivery
   actually works; manual dispatch revealed what the driver-assignment algorithm should be;
   doing support themselves produced real-time feedback. They personally emailed every new
   customer each night with a *personalized* note ("how were the chicken skewers from Oren's
   Hummus?").
4. Scale comes later. Automated dispatch and supply/demand matching only matter once you've
   found product-market fit — "you can go get the ice cream next time."

### Stanley's three takeaways
1. **Test your hypothesis** — treat ideas as experiments.
2. **Launch fast** — they shipped in under an hour with a trivial landing page.
3. **It's okay to do things that don't scale** — it's one of your biggest early advantages.

## Part 2 — Doing Things that Don't Scale, in Depth (Walker Williams)

> Definition: things that are fundamentally unsustainable — they won't bring in the millionth
> user. Usually it's time that breaks them. Three places to apply this: first users, turning
> users into champions, and finding product-market fit.

### Finding your first users
1. There is no silver bullet for user acquisition. The dream PPC campaign / springboard
   partnership / affiliate deal almost never exists. Companies with beautiful growth curves
   fought brutally for their first users.
2. It's the founder's job to do *whatever it takes* — 100 emails a day, cold calls, working
   your network (Stanford, YC). Expect to spend an hour and *not* get thousands of dollars
   back; ignore time-based ROI early on.
3. Think of it as pushing a boulder up a hill: steepest and hardest at the very start, then it
   steadies out until the boulder rolls on its own.
4. **Don't give the product away for free.** Free makes users treat it differently and gives
   you a false sense of security ("surely we can convert them later"). Make sure they value
   it. (Exceptions exist, but this is the default.)

### Turning users into Champions
1. A champion is a user who advocates for you. Every great growth strategy has them.
2. The easiest way to create one early is to delight users with an unusual, exceptional
   experience — and the simplest version of that is to **talk to users**, constantly, every
   day, for as long as possible. He's still the catch-all support email and does 12–20
   tickets a day; he reads every relevant tweet and community thread.
3. Three ways to talk to customers:
   - **Run support yourself.** (He and his co-founder did all of it up to ~$130–140k/month.)
     It's painful — opening the support portal still makes his stomach sink — but it tells you
     what to build and fix.
   - **Proactively reach out** to current, future, and *churned* users. Ask people who left
     why. Sometimes outreach alone keeps them; if not, you learn what to fix.
   - **Watch social media and communities.** Know how people talk about your brand and make
     bad experiences right.
4. Problems are inevitable; **always make it right.** One detractor can reverse the progress of
   ten champions. The customers who start out most frustrated often become the biggest
   champions — so eat the cost and fix the wrong-color, wrong-size, half-your-GMV order.

### Finding product-market fit
1. The product you launch with is almost never the product that scales — so iterate to PMF as
   fast as possible.
2. **Optimize for speed over scalability and clean code.** Resist the engineer's instinct to
   write beautiful, scalable code early. Example: to win a few enterprise customers, the CTO
   duplicated the codebase and database to ship a throwaway parallel product in 3–4 days
   instead of building it "right" in a month — then folded the proven features back in.
3. **Only worry about the next order of magnitude.** At 10 users, figure out how to get to
   100; at 100, think about 1,000. Necessity is the mother of invention.
4. Survive the breaking points. Teespring's site crashed nightly for months; the whole team
   slept with phones on loud under their pillows to restart servers. The technical debt and
   regret were worth it to hit PMF faster — those are just speed bumps.

### The overarching lesson
1. **Keep doing things that don't scale as long as humanly possible.** There's no magic
   moment — not the Series A, not a revenue milestone — when you stop. The moment you give it
   up, you hand smaller competitors who still do it the advantage. "It should be ripped from
   you."

## Part 3 — How to Get Press (Justin Kan)

### Mindset
1. Press is **not a meritocracy.** Journalists aren't out there discovering the best stories
   on merit; you have to manufacture and sell the story.
2. Set a goal first. "Wanting to be in the news" achieves nothing. Decide who you need to
   reach:
   - Socialcam → tech press, to be positioned as "video Instagram" for investors/influencers.
   - Exec (local cleaning) → SF-only local press; national coverage was useless.
   - Twitch → gaming-industry trades and game-dev blogs, to reach developers and advertisers.

### What counts as a story
1. Common story types: product launches, fundraising (press loves it), milestones/metrics,
   business profiles (usually once you're already successful), **stunts** (e.g., the YC
   company Wee dropping a block of ice with money frozen in it outside a PayPal dev
   conference), hiring announcements, and contributed/opinion articles.
2. The test: *if I weren't the founder, would I want to read this?* Your incremental 2.0.1
   feature release probably isn't a story.
3. It doesn't have to be original — just **"original enough."** Being the first console to
   raise $10M on Kickstarter (Ouya) is news; being the second cooler is not.

### The mechanics (treat it like a sales funnel)
1. **Come up with the story** (one of the types above).
2. **Get introduced**, don't cold-email. Best move: have a founder friend who was recently
   covered introduce you to the reporter who wrote about them. It costs the introducer
   nothing, and you inherit the reporter's trust by the transitive property.
3. **Give lead time** — reach out a week or more ahead. No one drops everything to cover your
   launch tomorrow unless you already have a relationship.
4. **Get them to invest time** via a face-to-face or at least a phone call (sunk-cost effect).
   An email-only exchange is the worst — easy to ignore and forget.
5. **Pitch from a script.** Write out your *ideal* published story as bullet points and
   memorize it; steer the conversation through that outline so they transcribe your version
   into the article.
6. **Follow up** a day or two before launch with an email containing all the collateral —
   video, screenshots, correct name spellings — with the important bits bolded. Then you ship
   and they publish simultaneously.

### PR firms and the bigger picture
1. Do PR yourself first. Firms only provide contacts and logistics — they can't tell you
   what's interesting about your company — and they're expensive ($5–20k/month), rarely worth
   it early.
2. Press is often a **vanity metric.** Coverage ≠ success — it doesn't mean users, revenue, or
   happy users. It's a fine way to get your first 100–1,000 users, but it is **not a scalable
   acquisition channel.** You'll run out of "new."
3. If you commit to it, keep a **heartbeat of news** — schedule launches at regular intervals
   so people don't forget you.
4. It's a **relationships business.** Maintain a few reporter relationships you can return to;
   they're invaluable if you ever need to get your side of a negative story out.
5. **Pay it forward** — help fellow founders get coverage (pass reporters other good story
   leads) and it comes back to you.
6. Case study — *Twitch Plays Pokémon*: the idea came from the community, not Twitch. But they
   gave it legs by setting the stage with prior coverage (so a BBC reporter Googling "Twitch"
   had context), making the company available to reporters, and pitching follow-up stories
   (when players beat the game, when the next version launched).

### Resources he recommended
1. Jason Calacanis's overview of getting press (former TechCrunch reporter).
2. *Trust Me, I'm Lying* (Ryan Holiday) — a "somewhat evil" but useful look at how stories
